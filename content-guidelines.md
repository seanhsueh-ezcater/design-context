# ezCater Content Guidelines

## Who We Are

ezCater was founded in August 2007 in Boston, Massachusetts, and is the only nationwide marketplace for corporate catering. We make it easy to order food online for your office — from routine office lunches to offsite client meetings, from 5 to 2,000 people. ezCater connects businesspeople with over 50,000 reliable local caterers and restaurants across the U.S.

---

## Our Content Strategy

### Mission
Articles will connect the challenges ezCater's audiences encounter at work to food while demonstrating the impact catering can have on their success.

### Audience Personas

**Office Admins** — Office Admins, Executive Assistants, Secretaries, and Office Managers. Constantly busy, managing multiple interests and personalities. Ordering food is always their third or fourth job.

**Sales** — Work across industries (pharma, tech, etc.). Constantly on the go, under pressure to meet quota. Orders food for client meetings in locations they may not know.

**Trainers** — Coordinating training across the country or within their office. Struggle with attendance, engagement, and measuring effectiveness. Ordering food is one more thing they don't have time for but can be key to engagement.

**Caterers** — Small restaurants, franchises, or dedicated catering shops. Managing back-of-house operations while building their business. May have expanded into catering as a market growth strategy.

## Brand Tone

ezCater's tone balances approachability and friendliness with knowledge and expertise. Casual but concise and exact.

Always be: **Helpful · Precise · Intelligent but down-to-earth · Conversational · Professional · Direct and clear**

Articles should always:
- Understand personas' issues at work and recognize their lack of time
- Be supported by reputable sources or experts
- Provide actionable advice
- Have the tone of a friendly mentor giving advice to a peer
- Be digestible and scannable
- Deliver specific advice that doesn't leave lingering questions

### Point of View
- Write in the present tense
- Use active voice at all times
- Use second- or third-person

## Style Guide

- Follow the **AP Stylebook** for style and spelling; fall back to Merriam-Webster
- Follow **AP Title Style** for article titles and subheads (e.g. *5 Alternatives to the Taco Bar*)
- Use the **Oxford comma**
- Always capitalize titles, whether before or after a person's name
- Use acronyms sparingly; spell out at first mention
- Use jargon only if necessary; define at first mention
- Content must be original and exclusive to ezCater — no plagiarism or copyrighted content
- Do not mention competitors or competitors' products
- Articles should be **500–700 words**

### Glossary
- **ezCater** (not EZcater or EzCater)
- **Stefania Mallett**
- Those who order catering: **customers** (not clients or guests)
- Refer to ezCater as an **"online app"**
- Do **not** use: *Seamless*

## Article Elements

### Takeaways
Every article includes 3 takeaways summarizing main points — helpful, actionable, and concise for readers on the go.

### Call to Action
Every article ends with a sentence driving readers to ezcater.com, relevant to the article topic.
> Example: *Looking to order for your next lunch and learn? Start your search here.*

### Social Post
Every article includes a 100-character social post for reader sharing. It should sum up the article, include a stat, or ask a question answered in the article.

---

## Links & Sourcing

### Sources
- Cite information accurately in the body of the content with inline links — no bibliography
- Include at least 1 ezCater article link within each article
- All data must come from reputable, non-competitor sources

**Acceptable sources:** Restaurant Business Magazine · Eater · Inc. · OfficeNinjas · ASAP · SHRM · Training Journal · Technomic · Forbes · Business Insider · Nation's Restaurant News · The New York Times

**Unacceptable sources:** Competitors (Grubhub, Seamless) · Crowdsourced sites (Quora, LiveStrong) · Small businesses

### Links
- Include 1–6 links total
- At least 1 ezCater article link, at least 1 strong external source
- Always link sources for any claims, stats, or data
- Use keywords as anchor text when linking to a site
- Use the source's name as anchor text when linking to a source

---

## Photos

### Featured Image
One relevant image at the top of every article. Must complement the article subject.

**Look for:** Candid and natural people · Human element · Diverse groups · Interesting angles, lighting, or depth of field

**Avoid:** Posed or "stock" looking images · Vector images · Illustrations · Heavily edited photos

All images should be horizontal with the subject in the middle third. Source from Shutterstock. Paste the image URL above the title with `Image:` before it.

**Alt text:** Short natural-language description, one sentence max. Include keyword if it fits naturally.

### Supplemental Images
Charts, diagrams, or photos that illustrate a point are encouraged. Must be license-free and sourced within the article.

---

## SEO Best Practices

### Keywords
- Use the primary keyword **3–6 times** in the body
- Use the primary keyword within the **first 100 characters**
- If a secondary keyword is assigned, use it **at least 3 times**

### Headlines
- Aim for **6–7 words**
- Use the keyword naturally
- Be descriptive, engaging, and honest — never promise what isn't in the article

---

## Formatting

**Subheads** — Break content into scannable sections. Brief, descriptive, can include keyword if natural.

**Lists** — Use numbered lists for steps, bulleted lists for groupings. Good for readability and search.

**Pull quotes** — Use for particularly important or standout quotes or data points.

---

## Tagging
Select 3–5 relevant tags per article. For specific topics, use tangentially related tags. For broad topics, use directly related tags.

## Product Copy Principles

Within our products, we're always consistent with ezCater's brand voice and tone standards, but we don't view them as a checklist. Some microcopy field labels should just say "ZIP code." Clever would be annoying.

When in doubt, these principles describe how we serve customers and advance business goals.

### Principle 1: Reinforce brand values by proving them
Product content focuses on advancing a customer successfully toward their goal. Deliver on the customer's intention, consistent with brand values — rather than speaking of brand values.

- Voice and tone serve action. Think of our four voice characteristics as tools, not restrictions.
- Part of going beyond helpful is saving customers time and focusing on their priorities.
- In a product, clever is rarely "ha ha," but might be a winking gesture toward the perfect solution.
- Speaking conversationally is real.
- Pros walk the walk, inspiring confidence through their words and behavior.

We advance the brand by demonstrating these characteristics, making good on our promises, and reinforcing trust.

### Principle 2: Write for the most particular audience and moment
Employ dynamic content when it's useful to the customer. Save them time and effort. If they've given us information, use it to their benefit. Be helpful and transparent, not creepy.

Don't make people interpret vague directions. If we must cover multiple cases, use clear labeling, enumeration, and meaningful differentiating details to help people know what applies to them and how to be successful.

### Principle 3: Aim for consistency, not uniformity
Use the standards until there's a problem they're not solving. Don't deviate from our standard terms, patterns, and styles unless you have good reason. There's enormous value in our consistency, for us and our audiences.

There are functional and stylistic reasons to deviate — know why you're doing it. The rules are guidelines, not laws. If we better serve a customer by deviating from a standard, bring it to the table and document the decision.

### Principle 4: Less really is usually more
Trust our customers and design patterns. Product content meets the needs of the customer at the moment they're using it, as validated by research, analytics, and content design best practices.

There's a tendency to ask "what if" about every possible scenario and to add qualifiers, adjectives, and tooltips "just in case" — but these additions need to prove their worth.

Excess content is BAD. Simplicity and brevity are challenging, but show our confidence. Excess content, especially if it's legalistic, introduces doubt and puts cognitive load on our customers.

### Principle 5: When more is needed, content design takes priority
"People don't read" isn't true if the content is properly designed. There are times we need to say more than fits in a field label or a single line of text — customers sometimes need and want more information.

In these cases, we still want to avoid the perception of "too much." We should not add walls of text. "More content" might mean:
- Two columns of three-word bullets
- An expandable section with more details
- Moving content to other parts of the page or flow to present it in its most useful context
